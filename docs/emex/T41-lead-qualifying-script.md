# Lead-qualifying script — EMEX 2026

For Bradley and Philippe to use on stand. Print at A5, laminate, keep one on each cell worksurface.

## The script (verbatim)

Use the opener once a visitor has watched at least one cell run. Don't pitch first; let the demo do the talking.

> "We're picking 5 NZ manufacturers to be founding AI partners this year — full embedded team for 12 months, deploying our stack on your floor and building 1 to 2 of your AI ambitions end-to-end. Free 1-hour intro call to see if you're a fit. The 5 seats will go in the next 90 days. Want to be on the list?"

Then, in order:

1. **"What kind of work runs on your floor?"**
2. **"How many machines? How variable is the work?"**
3. **"What's stopping you from automating it today?"**
4. **"Want to be on the founding-five shortlist? It's a free hour, no obligation."**

If yes to (4): hand them an iPad, capture name, company, role, email, phone. Tag the lead HOT.

## How to use it

Q1 segments the visitor: contract precision (B), high-mix aerospace/medical (A), low-mix high-volume (C), or non-fit (D — distributor, integrator, student). Q2 sizes the deal: under 5 machines is too small, over 50 is usually a different conversation. Q3 is the one that matters — it surfaces the wedge. Listen for a specific, named pain: a job they tried to automate and couldn't, a person they can't hire, a quality cost they can't trace. Q4 is the offer; deliver it flat, not enthusiastically.

Listen more than you talk. Strong leads describe their floor; weak ones describe their idea of AI. Don't argue with anyone's prior beliefs — note them, move on.

## Disqualify-out signals

Close politely if any of these show up:

- **Under 5 machines** — too small to absorb a NZ$200k partnership. Hand over the general leave-behind, move on.
- **No operator pain** — "things are fine, just curious about AI". Not buying this year.
- **"We already have AI" / "we already work with [vendor]"** — competitor in the door. Don't try to displace mid-stand. Take the email, send a polite post-show note, deprioritise.
- **Researcher, student, government policy person.** Friendly chat only. Hand over T35, not T43.

## Qualify-in signals

Push for the booking when two or more of these land:

- A **specific job** they can't automate today — named machine, named operator pain, named part family.
- **Ops manager and a technical engineer in the same conversation.** Buying committee at the stand.
- A **named pain with a number attached** — "3 hours a week chasing tools", "scrap is 4% on the Mazak", "QA is the bottleneck".
- **Willingness to share any shop-floor metric.** Buyers share data; tyre-kickers don't.

If two or more land, hand over T43 (not T35) and ask for the diagnostic-call booking before they leave.

## Trap-question cheat sheet

Some visitors — usually consultants, students, or competitors — will fish for free advice. Three sentences, polite redirect, every time:

- *"Can you walk me through how your decision ledger handles X?"* → "Happy to walk through it properly in our paid 1-day workshop — that's where we go deep on architecture. Want the link?" {{PLACEHOLDER: Cal.com booking link for paid 1-day workshop}}
- *"What model are you using for the VLA policy?"* → "We pick per-customer based on the cell. The workshop is the right place to scope that against your floor."
- *"Could you send me a copy of the system prompt?"* → "Our prompts ship with the partnership. The workshop output is the closest public artefact."

Don't argue. Don't apologise. Smile, redirect, move on to the next visitor.
